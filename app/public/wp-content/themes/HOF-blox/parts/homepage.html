How was the game of tennis able to expand from the Country Clubs to the Public Parks and Public Schools in St Louis and nationally?

Dwight Davis, St. Louis's visionary Park Commissioner, played a pivotal role. He spearheaded the construction of tennis courts in St. Louis City parks, making tennis accessible to all citizens. In 1923, he also served as President of the United States Lawn Tennis Association and later held an impressive political career, serving as Secretary of War in President Calvin Coolidge's cabinet from 1925 to 1929.

What role did a St Louisan play in increasing the popularity of tennis throughout the world?

Dwight Davis conceived of and started the international tennis competition known as the Davis Cup. Today over 130 countries compete for the Davis Cup which has brought Nations together and increased the popularity of tennis worldwide.

Why was St Louis so instrumental in the birth of professional Tennis?

The Capps and Holton Families with the help of Earl “Butch” Buchholz sponsored some of the first pro tennis tournaments here in St Louis. With prize money of $10,000 to $19,000 these tournaments attracted the top players in the world and Professional Tennis was born. Butch than participated in a worldwide tour with 8 players known as the “The Handsome Eight”. This group brought pro tennis to the world.

How did thousands of St Louisans come to learn and play the game of tennis?

Richard Hudlin, Earl Buchholz Sr., Alan Carvel and a host of other great teachers and mentors fanned out over the public parks and schools of St Louis and brought the great game of tennis to the masses. St Louis became one of the most active tennis cities in the Country.

How did a cold, dimly lit military building with five tennis courts laid out on a slick wood floor on Market Street in the City of St Louis produce over 500 national and international tennis titles which include 22 Grand Slam singles and doubles titles?

At the 138 Infantry Armory in the 1950’s and 60’s some of the greatest tennis players in the history of the sport shared a wood drill floor with the military. Because the building housed the only 5 indoor courts in St Louis and all the players had to come together to practice champions were born. Sometimes in sports the best competitors come from difficult environments and that was certainly true at the St Louis Armory.

In what year did St Louis have three women ranked in the top 10 nationally?

In 1964 Carol Hanks Aucamp, Mary Ann Eisel Beattie and Justina Bricka were all ranked in the top 10 National Women’s Tennis Rankings. All three women played and trained together and with the male players at the Armory.

Why would Pro Tennis not be where it is today without a great St Louis Tennis Champion and Pioneer?

Earl “Butch” Buchholz along with being a great player and one of the first touring pros was a visionary who saw the potential of professional tennis long before others. Butch has played key roles in both amateur and professional tennis and early bin 1963 he was a founding member of the first men’s player association.He was a pioneer in developing both man’s and women’s tennis, with expertise in fund raising, sponsorship and marketing to help create more tennis viewers throughout the world.

How in 1968 was St Louis able to dominate the Boy’s National Junior Championships in Kalamazoo Michigan with singles titles in both the 18 and under and the 16 and under.

Bob McKinley and Jimmy Connors dominated the courts in Kalamazoo, Michigan in early August of 1968 by winning both the boys 18 and under title and the boys 16 and under title respectively. Bob for north St. Louis County and Jimmy from East St Louis trained together at the St Louis Armory. To have two players from the same City win both titles in the same day was an amazing achievement.

How in 1975 could a young man from East St Louis play a Sumner High School graduate for the men’s singles title at Wimbledon and forever change the game of tennis?

Jimmy Connors and Arthur Ashe met in the finals of Wimbledon in 1975. Arthur became the first black man to win Wimbledon that year. Both Arthur and Jimmy trained and played at the Armory. The slick wood floors prepared both players for the fast grass courts at the All-England Club. Arthur won three Grand Slam titles in singles and two in doubles. Ashe was the first Black player selected to the United States Davis Cup team, and the only Black man ever to win the singles titles at Wimbledon, the US Open, and the Australian Open. Ashe was a great tennis champion but he was a champion for social justice off the court. Connors held the top Association of Tennis Professionals ranking for a then-record 160 consecutive weeks from 1974 to 1977 and a career total of 268 weeks. He finished with 8 Grand Slam titles and a record 109 Tournament titles.

Who from St Louis was one of the greatest men’s doubles players of all time?

Ken Flach grew up in St Louis, played at SIUE and became one of the greatest doubles played of all time. Ken was the owner of 3 NCAA championships, 34 pro doubles titles, two US Open Men’s Double titles, two Wimbledon Men’s Doubles titles, a French Open and Wimbledon Mixed doubles title, an 11-2 Davis Cup record and an Olympic Gold Medal in Seoul South Korea.

What role did the St Louis Armory play in the tennis career of the greatest senior tennis player of all time?

Jimmy Parker grew up in St Louis in a great tennis family. His Father Ward, his mother Merceina and his bother Larry have all been inducted into he St Louis Tennis Hall of Fame. Jimmy played at the Armory with all the great St Louis players in the 1960’s. Jimmy’s love for the game of tennis and the skill’s he honed on the wood floors of the Armory took him on a seventy-year tennis journey. Jimmy Parker possesses 153 gold balls representing 153 USTA national championships and 22 International titles.

Early Years and Tennis Expansion

1900s - 1920s

In the early 1900s, particularly in 1904, Dwight Davis, the St. Louis Park Commissioner, embarked on a visionary mission to make tennis accessible to all citizens. He built numerous tennis courts in St. Louis City parks, pioneering the idea of public access to the game. This initiative soon spread across the nation, influencing the expansion of tennis from exclusive country clubs to public parks and schools.

The Birth of the Davis Cup

Not only did Dwight Davis advocate for local accessibility, but he also made a global impact. In 1900, he conceived and initiated the Davis Cup, an international tennis competition that brought nations together and significantly elevated the sport's popularity worldwide. Today, over 130 countries compete for this prestigious cup.

St. Louis and the Birth of Professional Tennis

1930s - 1950s

During the mid-20th century, particularly in the 1930s to 1950s, the Capps and Holton Families, supported by Earl “Butch” Buchholz, sponsored some of the earliest professional tennis tournaments in St. Louis. These tournaments, featuring substantial prize money, attracted top players from around the world and marked the birth of Professional Tennis. Butch's later involvement in a global tour with "The Handsome Eight" further propelled the professionalization of the sport.

Tennis for the Masses

St. Louisans like Richard Hudlin, Earl Buchholz Sr., and Alan Carvel played pivotal roles in democratizing tennis. They spread the game to public parks and schools across the city, transforming St. Louis into one of the most active tennis communities in the nation. Their dedication and mentorship brought tennis to thousands, making it a beloved sport for the masses.

The Armory: A Birthplace of Champions

1960s - 1970s

During the 1950s and 60s, particularly in the 1950s and 1960s, the 138 Infantry Armory, despite its cold and dimly lit setting, housed a wooden drill floor that incubated some of the greatest tennis players in history. The lack of indoor courts in St. Louis compelled players to practice together, fostering a competitive environment. This unique setting nurtured champions and provided a strong foundation for their successful careers.

Women's Tennis Triumph

In 1964, St. Louis witnessed a remarkable achievement in women's tennis as Carol Hanks Aucamp, Mary Ann Eisel Beattie, and Justina Bricka all achieved top 10 rankings in the National Women’s Tennis Rankings. Their collaboration and training at the Armory exemplified the supportive community and dedication to tennis excellence in St. Louis.
